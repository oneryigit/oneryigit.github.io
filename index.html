Welcome!

I am a Ph.D. candidate in political science at Binghamton University. I expect to complete my doctorate by May 2025.

My research interests are centered around ethnic and religious minorities, state minority policies, ethnic conflict, nation-building, and human rights, with a particular focus on the Middle East and Southern Europe. I am interested in the struggle for recognition of ethnic minorities in Turkey, Spain, Bulgaria, and Greece.

Methodologically, my research incorporates a range of tools and software. Alongside conventional statistical and qualitative methods, I am also employing text analysis and geospatial data analysis in my research. My GitHub page contains some of my Python notebooks for my text and geospatial data analysis, feel free to fork.

Dissertation Project

My dissertation project investigates how third-wave democracies vary in their treatment of ethnic minorities, specifically exploring why the struggles for recognition are more successful in some states than others. This research aims to identify and understand the factors that lead to these divergent outcomes. I am currently compiling a novel, large-N database of state minority policies and conducting comparative case studies.

The concept of the struggle for recognition is central to social and political theory and is intricately linked with broader discussions on identity, rights, and social justice. This struggle is particularly evident in the political sphere, where minority groups strive to secure equal rights and recognition for their unique cultural, linguistic, and historical identities. Normatively, mutual recognition is recognized as fundamental to human identity and essential for individual development and self-realization. However, when misrecognition occurs, it can lead to harm and act as a form of oppression, impacting the group’s ability to participate fully in society.

Despite the critical nature of these issues, empirical research into what it takes for a minority group to achieve recognition remains sparse. My dissertation addresses this gap by examining the institutional frameworks that either facilitate or impede such recognition, as well as exploring the role of civil society and social movements in advocating for these groups. It conducts a comprehensive empirical analysis to uncover the specific factors and strategies that support the recognition of ethnic groups in diverse socio-political contexts.
